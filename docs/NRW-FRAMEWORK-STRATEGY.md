**For:** Amplified / Turner & Townsend bid and product thinking  
**Re:** Find a Tender notice 078212-2025, NRW Digital, Data and Technology Services Framework  
**Style:** UK English, no em dashes, direct and practical.

## 1. The timing reality, stated plainly

This notice closed at Stage 1 on 5 January 2026 and the award is estimated at 29 May 2026. So this document is not about winning entry to this framework now. It is about:

1. **Stage 2, if you are shortlisted.** The tool is a strong dialogue and presentation asset right now (section 7).
2. **The framework reopening.** The open framework reopens once, roughly six months before the end of year 3, with the new term running years 4 to 8. That is a real re-entry point around 2028, by which time the tool can carry genuine deployment evidence.
3. **Every other framework with the same four scored themes.** The WPSQ themes here (relevant experience, technical and professional ability, capacity and resources, sustainability and innovation) recur across Welsh and UK public procurement. The drafts in [WPSQ-DRAFTS.md](./WPSQ-DRAFTS.md) are built as reusable templates.

Treat the WPSQ drafts as a library you reuse, not a one-off for a closed deadline.

## 2. Value verdict, by party

**For entering this framework: low.** The framework competition assesses you as a supplier, not as a squad. There is no service phase, no team to compose, no Service Standard assessment in scope at Stage 1. The tool's core inputs are not what the WPSQ asks for. And with cost weighted at 50 per cent, capability differentiation only ever competes for the other half of the score. Anyone who tells you a team-fit tool wins a framework-entry bid is overfitting it.

**For winning call-offs once on the framework: high.** This is the real home. Once appointed, you compete in mini-competitions against the other four to seven suppliers for actual services. The framework scope explicitly includes digital service design, agile delivery and coaching, and capability uplift. Those call-offs will ask you to propose a team for a real service at a real phase, often assessed against the Wales standard. That is exactly what the tool does.

**For de-risking delivery and retaining the account: high.** Winning a call-off is the start. Keeping the account over eight years means delivering with visible rigour. The readiness, rigour and evidence layers do that.

**For the client, NRW: potentially the highest value of all.** NRW is standing up a £72m, eight-year, five-supplier framework and has named capability uplift and agile coaching as in-scope services. Running a framework that size has a governance problem: across many suppliers and dozens of call-offs over eight years, how does NRW assure that the teams suppliers field are the right shape, meet the Wales standard, and practise agile with rigour rather than asserting it? Their own stated risks include shortages in specialist skills and dependency on external partners. The tool answers exactly that. Reframed for NRW, it is a capability and assurance instrument, and it is itself in scope of the framework.

## 3. The conflict you must resolve first

The notice records that a conflicts assessment has been prepared. This matters because the two highest-value plays appear to collide:

- If you **bid for call-offs** on the framework, you are a competing supplier.
- If you also **sell NRW a tool that evaluates call-off submissions**, you are influencing the process you compete in. That is a serious conflict and could taint both the tool and your standing.

Do not try to do both in the same form. The clean resolution, which is also the stronger story, is this:

**Position the client-facing tool as a capability-uplift deliverable that NRW owns and operates, not an evaluation service you run over your competitors.** You build NRW's own assurance capability and hand it over. NRW runs it. You do not see competitors' submissions through it. This sidesteps the conflict entirely, and it lands precisely on the Amplified ethos of building client capability rather than dependency. It is also exactly the kind of thing the framework's capability-uplift line is meant to buy.

So the tool has two clean, separated identities:

- **Internal:** a call-off and delivery-assurance accelerator your delivery teams use, never shown to NRW as an evaluation engine.
- **External:** a capability-uplift product NRW owns and runs for its own framework governance, delivered and handed over by you.

Keep these separated in every conversation and in any conflicts declaration.

## 4. The repositioning, three plays ranked

**Play A, the call-off accelerator (build first, lowest risk).** Internal tool. Feed in a call-off requirement, get the best-achievable team shape, the readiness against the Wales standard, the agile rigour score, and the draft capability and approach narrative. This is where the bid-ingestion engine actually belongs: pointed at call-off specifications, not framework WPSQs. Wins mini-competitions and de-risks delivery. No conflict, immediate value the moment you are on a framework.

**Play B, the capability-uplift product (highest client value, needs the conflict firewall).** Client-owned tool delivered as a capability-uplift engagement. NRW runs it to specify call-offs consistently, set team and standard expectations, and self-assure delivery across suppliers. Directly addresses NRW's skills-shortage and supplier-dependency risks. In scope of the framework, so it is a sellable service line, not just a sales aid. Requires the ownership and operation to sit with NRW (section 3).

**Play C, the multi-authority assurance product (the scale play, later).** The GDS, DDaT and Wales standards are national. Other contracting authorities run similar frameworks with the same governance problem. Once proven with one client, the tool generalises into a product you offer beyond NRW. This is the long game and the reason to build it cleanly now.

Recommended sequence: build A, prove it on real call-offs to accrue genuine evidence, then offer B to NRW as a capability-uplift engagement, then generalise to C. Each stage earns the right to the next and produces the evidence that makes the next bid honest.

## 5. What this changes in the build

The build sheets you have are sound, but the priorities shift:

- **Retarget the bid layer at call-offs, not framework WPSQs.** The tender-ingestion and question-mapping work is right, but the unit of work is a call-off specification with a named service and phase, not a supplier questionnaire. Adjust the demo and the language accordingly.
- **Pull the portfolio and assurance view forward.** It was deferred. For Play B it is central: NRW needs the cross-call-off, cross-supplier view, not a single engagement. Build the engagement-level tool first, but design the data model for portfolio rollup from day one (the schema already supports this).
- **Make client-ownership and handover first-class.** Play B only works if NRW can run the tool without you. That raises the bar on explainability, the capability-uplift framing, and a clean handover and training path. This aligns with build capability not dependency and is a social-value asset (section 6).
- **Keep the conflict firewall in the architecture.** The internal call-off mode and the client-owned assurance mode should be separable deployments with separate data, so there is never a path by which you see competitors' submissions through a client instance.
- **Welsh language and the wellbeing duty are not edge cases here, they are the differentiator.** The tool already treats Welsh language capability and the future generations duty as first-class. For this specific client that is the sharpest point of distinction. Lead with it.

Price discipline still wins half the game. The tool is a quality and social-value differentiator. It does not touch the 50 per cent cost weighting, so it sits alongside a competitive commercial model, it does not replace one.

## 7. Stage 2, the live use right now

If you are shortlisted, Stage 2 includes an outline technical proposal, a presentation that is not scored but supports clarification during dialogue, and structured dialogue before final tenders.

The presentation being unscored is not a reason to underuse it. It is the moment to shape the dialogue in your favour. A short, working demonstration of the tool, run on a realistic NRW-style call-off scenario, does three things no slide can: it proves your method is real and operationalised, it shows fluency with the Wales standard including Welsh language and the wellbeing duty, and it gives the panel a concrete artefact to remember you by during dialogue. Demonstrate it as your method made tangible, framed as capability you would help NRW build, never as an evaluation engine you would run over other suppliers. That framing keeps the conflict firewall intact while making the strongest possible impression.

## 8. Decisions for you

Two forks worth settling deliberately, because they change what gets built and how it is sold.

First, which buyer leads: your own delivery teams (Play A, an internal accelerator) or NRW's digital leadership (Play B, a client-owned assurance product). Build A first regardless, but the answer decides where the second push goes and how hard you press the capability-uplift line in dialogue.

Second, how firmly you draw the conflict firewall. The cleanest position is that any client-facing instance is owned and operated by NRW and never sees competitor submissions. Confirm that this is the line, because it should be stated consistently in any conflicts declaration and it shapes the architecture.

One honest closing point. The tool does not win you this framework, and a tool that promised to would be the wrong tool. What it does is win the work the framework exists to award, de-risk the delivery that keeps the account, and, handled cleanly, become a capability-uplift product the client itself wants to buy. That is a stronger and more durable position than a bid gimmick, and it is true.
